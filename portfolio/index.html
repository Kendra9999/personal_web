About

My research interests focus on surgical video perception and medical image registration. Below is the introduction about my work on augmented reality displays in surgical video perception.

AR technique - To See What Is Unseen

Recent years have witnessed the rapid emergence of minimally invasive surgery (MIS) due to its short recovery time, less tissue trauma, low infection rate and low post-operative complication rate over traditional open surgery. According to the statistics from iData Research, there are more than 13 million cases of laparoscopic MIS performed every year around the world. In laparoscopic MIS, a laparoscope is inserted into the abdominal cavity through a trocar, allowing surgeons to obtain visual information of the surgical scene. However, the field of view is highly limited by the laparoscopic viewing angle, making it hard for surgeons to accurately locate crucial unseen anatomical structures, such as blood vessels, tumors and lesions. When conducting complex surgical maneuvers, surgeons need to imagine the intra-operative target location through an electronic screen displaying the laparoscopic vision field, which requires adequate surgical experience and visuo-spatial awareness.

To tackle these issues, it is necessary to construct intelligent robot-assisted surgical navigation systems to guide and assist surgeons in MIS. One important task of such surgical navigation systems is to achieve intra-operative augmented reality (AR). AR technique can facilitate the fusion of both pre-operative and intra-operative information, and enhance the perception capacity of the surgical scene, leading to more effective and safer surgical outcomes. To achieve this, intra-operative AR aims to accurately superimpose pre-operative virtual models obtained from computed tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance (MR) images onto intra-operative laparoscopic views, which can be realized through dense stereo 3D reconstruction followed by point cloud registration. Specifically, the goal of dense stereo 3D reconstruction is to estimate the depth maps for target soft tissues in the surgical scene from the endoscope camera viewpoint with high accuracy and fidelity.

However, it is still challenging to achieve precise AR display due to low accuracy of depth estimation in surgical environment. One of the keys to tackle this issue is to effectively learn complementary spatial and temporal information for depth estimation from the complex spatiotemporal dynamics in surgical videos. In this work, we propose a novel spatiotemporal U-shaped network for self-supervised depth estimation. Experimental results on two publicly available datasets demonstrate that the proposed network achieves better results than the state-of-the-art self-supervised depth estimation methods. We further develop an automatic dense stereo 3D reconstruction framework to estimate 3D point clouds of target organs from surgical videos. At last, we use the estimated 3D point clouds to register 3D models obtained from pre-operative 3D images with the surgical scenes, aiming to provide AR display. On two in-house datasets, we validate the efficacy of the proposed framework. Results from our comprehensive validation experiments demonstrate that our method can achieve accurate and precise AR display for intra-operative surgical scenes.
